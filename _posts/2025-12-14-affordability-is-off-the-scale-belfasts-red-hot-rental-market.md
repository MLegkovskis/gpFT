**Affordability is off the scale**: Belfast's red-hot rental market
================================================================================

**Belfast's rental market is experiencing unprecedented growth, with yields surpassing the UK average and rents reaching record highs**. According to recent data, the city's average rental yield is between 6% and 8%, with some areas reaching yields of up to 10%. This growth is driven by a combination of factors, including a shortage of supply, increasing demand, and rising property prices.

The current rental yield in Belfast is significantly higher than the UK average, which stands at around 6%. This disparity has made Belfast an attractive destination for investors, with many seeking to capitalize on the city's growing rental market. However, this influx of investment has also led to concerns about affordability, with many locals struggling to keep up with rising rents.

According to [Wise](https://wise.com/us/blog/what-is-a-good-rental-yield-uk), Belfast's city-centre rental yield is around 8.1%, while the overall yield is approximately 7.6%. Similarly, [John Minnis Estate Agents](https://theintermediary.co.uk/2025/08/belfast-tops-uk-rental-yield-forecasts-as-northern-ireland-homes-remain-44-cheaper-than-average/) reports an average yield of around 6.1% across the city.

The UK-wide average rental yield is around 6%, with regional averages ranging from 5.5% to 6.5%. According to [Zoopla](https://www.zoopla.co.uk/discover/property-news/best-buy-to-let-locations/), the national average yield is around 5.8% to 6%, with the North West and Scotland topping the list.

### Narrative comparison

The current rental market in Belfast is often compared to the 2008 housing crisis. However, the key differences between the two periods are:

* **Price level**: The average monthly rent has risen from roughly £950 in 2008 to £1,130 in 2025, a 19% increase in real terms.
* **Growth trajectory**: Unlike the 2008-2010 period, when rents declined and only recovered slowly, the current market shows continuous double-digit growth.
* **Market tightness**: Vacancy is now around 2%, comparable to the low-vacancy peak of the crisis but with higher rents.
* **Homelessness pressure**: The current number of people seeking emergency accommodation is the highest since 2008, indicating that the affordability squeeze is at least as severe as during the crisis.
